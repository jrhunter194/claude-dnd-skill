# The Falling Stars

*An original D&D 5e adventure for the `dm` skill. A frontier mystery that becomes a
cosmic emergency. For 3–5 characters, starting at level 1 and ending near level 5.*

- **System:** D&D 5e (compatible with both the 2014 and 2024 rulesets)
- **Structure:** Three acts. Act II is hub-and-spoke — the four star-fall sites can be
  approached in any order. Acts I and III are anchored.
- **Tone:** Folk-cosmic horror. A muddy frontier town, a strange light in the sky, and a
  thing in the dark that is *singing*. Dread over gore; wonder over dread.
- **Pillars:** Investigation and exploration carry the weight; combat punctuates rather
  than dominates; social play decides who the party can trust.

---

## Running This Adventure

The party arrives in the frontier town of **Hollowmere** in the days after a star fell
from the sky and buried itself in the **Saltfen** marsh. What looks like a single strange
event is the first tremor of a slow catastrophe: the sky is coming apart, and pieces of it
are landing one by one across the **Reach of Hollowmere**.

This document is the source the DM works from. It is written to be read top-to-bottom once,
then mined per-scene. Boxed passages marked **READ-ALOUD** are player-facing; everything
else is for the DM. Stat blocks reference standard 5e SRD monsters by name (e.g. *cult
fanatic*, *swarm of insects*) — look them up with `lookup.py` at the table rather than
reproducing them here. Where the adventure invents a creature, a stat note is given inline.

**The central mystery** — *what is actually falling, and does it mean us harm?* — must stay
alive to the end. Do not let the party (or the DM) settle the answer early. The pieces of
evidence are seeded so that an attentive party can form a theory by the end of Act II, be
genuinely unsure whether they are right, and have that theory tested at the climax.

## Background — The Truth Behind the Sky

*DM-only. The players should discover this in fragments, never as exposition.*

Ages ago, in the war the elves only half-remember, a celestial guardian was killed in the
heavens. Its name, where any record survives, is **Astera, the Sky-Warden** — a vault-keeper
who once held a single boundary closed against the things outside it. When Astera was broken,
its body did not fall. It *drifted*, a slow silver cloud of fragments turning in the high
dark, each shard carrying a sliver of a single shattered mind.

The elven astronomers of the old **Lantern Order** found the largest surviving shard and,
fearing what a waking fragment might do, bound it beneath their mountain observatory — the
**Orrery** — and kept watch over it for centuries. The Order dwindled. The watch lapsed. The
binding, untended, has begun to fail.

A failing binding *sings*. The bound shard has started to call the rest of Astera's scattered
body down out of the sky. The falling stars are those shards answering. Each one that lands
tries, blindly, to do the only thing it knows how to do: **rebuild a body** — reaching into
whatever lies nearby (corpses, livestock, stone, standing water, the minds of the susceptible)
and reshaping it toward Astera's lost form. The result is **starspawn**: wrong, half-made
horrors that the shard discards as failures, and a low psychic *song* that the desperate, the
grieving, and the faithful can hear at the edge of sleep.

**The mystery's real answer — author this as fixed, independent of any player guess.** Astera
was neither god nor monster. It was a *door-warden*, and it was killed precisely because it
would not open the door. The shards are not evil. But a mind reassembled from fragments by
people who do not understand it will not come back as the warden it was — it will come back as
whatever its gatherers shape it into. The Choir wants a god. If they get their way, they will
make one: a half-mad celestial that remembers only that it was murdered, and remembers nothing
of what it was guarding *against*. That is the danger. Not the stars. The reconvergence.

This means **all three of the party's likely end-state choices are coherent**, and none is
clearly correct — which is the point:
- **Destroy the shards** — silence Astera for good. Safe, final, and a small murder of
  something that may have been the only thing holding a worse door shut.
- **Complete the reconvergence carefully** — and gamble everything on *who* does the shaping.
- **Re-bind the shard** — kick the catastrophe down the road for the next Lantern Order to
  inherit, exactly as the last one did.

## The Three Truths

The compact form the DM keeps in `world.md`:

1. **Settlement —** *Hollowmere*, a fading frontier town on the edge of the Saltfen marsh,
   beneath the Pale Tarns. Mud, peat-smoke, and a stubborn people who have learned not to ask
   the mountains for favors.
2. **Threat —** *The sky is coming apart.* Shards of a dead celestial are falling across the
   Reach, and where they land the dead don't rest and the living start to hum a tune they
   never learned. A doomsday cult, the **Choir of the Open Sky**, is racing to gather them.
3. **Mystery —** *What is Astera, and what does the Choir intend to wake?* The answer the
   party reaches will decide whether they finish the catastrophe or start a new one.

## Threat Escalation Arc

Map the five stages onto the acts. The DM advances the stage as beats land.

| Stage | Trigger | What the world looks like |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Rumor | Act I opens | One star fell. People are spooked; the marsh is "off." Nobody connects it to anything. |
| 2 — Footprint | Act I climax | The crash site is *wrong*. Starspawn. The song. A cultist, watching. |
| 3 — Spread | Act II | More stars fall across the Reach. Each site is a small disaster. The Choir is everywhere first. |
| 4 — Convergence | Act II climax | The Choir has enough shards. The Orrery's old binding is openly failing. The sky is loud. |
| 5 — Reconvergence | Act III | The reassembly begins at the Orrery. Whatever the party allows to wake, wakes. |

## Factions

**The Choir of the Open Sky** — a doomsday cult who believe the falling stars are the pieces
of a returning god and that gathering them will earn the gatherers a place at its side. Most
Choristers are not monsters; they are the grieving, the sick, and the overlooked, who heard
the song and were *answered* by it for the first time in their lives. Their leader, **Canticle
Vehr**, knows more than the rank and file and believes it anyway. The Choir is always at each
star-fall site first. *Archetype: apocalyptic cult. Activity in Act II: racing the party shard
to shard. Relationship to party: opposed, but recruitable individuals exist.*

**The Lantern Order** — the all-but-extinct remnant of the elven astronomers who bound the
shard beneath the Orrery. Reduced now to one stubborn scholar, **Magister Oraine Vesk**, who
knows the binding is failing and is terrified of both the Choir *and* of what a clumsy
"rescue" of the sky could unleash. Wants the shards contained, not destroyed, not awakened —
and is not certain that's possible. *Archetype: dwindling keepers of a dangerous secret.
Activity: hoarding lore, hiding. Relationship: wary ally if earned.*

**The Reach Authority (Hollowmere's town)** — Reeve **Halda Crane** speaks for a frightened
town that wants the problem gone and is not particular about how. Practical, exhausted, no
patience for cosmic talk. Will back the party while they produce results and turn on them the
moment they look like the cause. *Archetype: frightened local power. Activity: damage control.
Relationship: conditional employer.*

**The Faint Voice** — not a faction so much as a presence: a single shard that fell *coherent*,
landing not as a monster but bound up in a living child, **Wren**, who now speaks sometimes
with two voices. Whether the Voice is Astera's better self reaching for help, or a lure, is
the sharpest edge of the mystery. *Relationship: unknown, and meant to stay that way.*

# Act I — The First Star

*Levels 1–2. Anchored. Goal: pull the party from "strangers passing through" to "the only
people who understand what's actually happening," and end on the first real glimpse of the
horror under the wonder.*

## Chapter 1.1 — The Light Over the Saltfen

**Starting situation.** The party is in Hollowmere — passing through, hired on, or run
aground here for reasons of their own. Three nights ago, something fell out of the sky into
the marsh with a sound like a bell struck underwater, and a light that turned the low clouds
silver for a heartbeat. Since then the Saltfen has been *wrong*: the frogs have gone silent,
two trappers haven't come back, and old Hewe swears he hears singing off the water at night.

> **READ-ALOUD.** Hollowmere is a town built on the idea that the marsh can be argued with.
> Plank walkways ride above black water; peat-smoke hangs in the reeds; every roofline leans a
> little, as if listening to something out past the edge of the lamplight. Three nights ago the
> sky cracked open and dropped a star into the Saltfen, and the town has not slept right since.
> The frogs have stopped. And if you stand at the marsh's edge after dark and hold very still,
> you can almost — *almost* — make out a tune.

**The hook.** Reeve **Halda Crane** is offering coin to anyone willing to walk into the marsh
and find out what fell — and find the two missing trappers, Edda and her son Pell, while
they're at it. She frames it as a practical job. She does not say the word *star* if she can
help it.

**What's really going on (DM).** The shard is half-sunk in a peat hollow a half-day into the
Saltfen. It has begun rebuilding: the trappers are dead, and the shard has been *using* them.
The "singing" is real and is the shard's call. A pair of Choir scouts is already in the marsh,
having tracked the fall — the party is not the first to come looking.

**Hollowmere — people and places worth a scene:**
- **Reeve Halda Crane** — town reeve. Tired, fair, transactional. Verbal tic: counts things on
  her fingers when she's deciding whether to trust you. *Stat note: commoner; treat as noble
  for any social-leverage purposes.*
- **The Gull & Tallow** — the town's one inn. **Hewe**, the old potman, is the first source of
  the "singing" rumor and will, if plied with drink, mention that the *last* time the
  Lantern folk's mountain "sang," his grandmother's village emptied overnight. (Foreshadow:
  the Orrery, the Lantern Order, the cyclical nature of this.)
- **Sash**, a marsh-guide child of ten who knows the Saltfen better than anyone and will offer
  to lead the party for a price they'll regret negotiating. *Use Sash to deliver geography and
  to be in danger later.*
- **The shrine-without-a-god** — a weathered standing stone at the marsh edge, Lantern Order
  work, carved with a closed eye and a ring of stars. A successful **DC 13 Religion or History**
  check reads it as a *ward*, not a shrine — something here was meant to be kept *out*, or
  *down*. (Seed of the mystery.)

**Beats for 1.1:**
- The party accepts the job, or is maneuvered into it (the missing trappers' kin, a debt to the
  Reeve, simple curiosity).
- The party learns the "singing" is real, not superstition — at least one of them hears it.
- The party sets out into the Saltfen.

**Telegraph for the crash site:** before they reach it, give them the *failed drafts*. A heron
with too many joints, standing motionless. Reeds bent into a spiral all pointing one way. A dead
fox, perfectly preserved, its eyes replaced with two beads of silver metal. The shard is
*practicing*. None of it attacks. All of it is wrong.

## Chapter 1.2 — What Fell

**The crash site.** A peat hollow, the standing water skinned with a film of silver. At the
center, half-buried, a shard of **skyfall iron** the size of a cart — black shot through with
veins of moving light, warm to the touch, humming at a pitch that sits behind the teeth. The
song is loud here. The two trappers are here too. They are dead, and they are *standing*.

> **READ-ALOUD.** The hollow opens ahead of you and the song stops being a rumor. It is in your
> molars. It is in the water, which lies too still and shines like a coin. And at the center,
> jutting from the black peat, is a piece of something that was never meant to be this close to
> the ground: a slab of dark metal veined with light that *crawls*, slow, beneath its surface
> like sparks under skin. Two figures stand knee-deep in the water before it, facing away from
> you. One of them is wearing Edda the trapper's coat.

**The encounter.** Edda and Pell are now **starspawn husks** — the shard's discarded first
drafts, animate and hostile. Treat each as a **zombie** with these changes: when one drops to
0 HP it bursts into a brief chime of silver light and a swarm of biting motes (treat as a one-
turn *swarm of insects* in its space that disperses at the end of the next round). They are
slow, relentless, and horribly familiar to anyone who knew the trappers — play the horror of
*who* they are, not just the threat.

**The complication — the Choir scouts.** Two **cultists** (use *cultist*, SRD) are watching
from a blind upslope, having arrived first. They do not announce themselves. The DM chooses,
based on the party's approach:
- If the party is loud and direct, the scouts slip away to report — and the party finds a
  *Choir sign* scratched into a dead tree (an open eye ringed with stars — the inversion of the
  ward-stone's *closed* eye; an attentive player should clock the deliberate opposition).
- If the party is careful, they can catch or overhear the scouts and learn the first hard fact:
  **other people know about this, they have a name for it ("a Tear of the Vault"), and they are
  glad it fell.**

**The shard itself.** It cannot be moved by hand (immensely heavy; *and* touching it bare-skinned
forces a **DC 12 Wisdom save** or the toucher hears, for one clear second, a single voice in the
song — grieving, not malicious — and takes no damage but is *shaken*: disadvantage on their next
check as it lingers). A chunk can be broken off with effort (this becomes a plot object — see
**Skyfall Iron**, appendix). Destroying the whole shard is beyond the party's means at level 1;
that impossibility is the point, and it sets up Act III.

**Act I climax beat.** As the husks fall and the motes disperse, the song *changes* — for a
moment it resolves into something almost like a word, almost like a name, and every PC feels it
reach for them. Then a child's voice, somewhere in the reeds, answers it. The party turns — and
sees **Wren** (Chapter 2 hook) standing at the hollow's edge, humming the same tune, unafraid,
eyes catching the silver light. Then the moment breaks, the song drops back to a hum, and the
marsh is just a marsh again. End the session here if you can.

**Beats for 1.2 (mark complete as they land):**
- The party confronts what fell and survives the husks.
- The party learns the Choir exists and *wants* this.
- The party meets, or glimpses, the Star-Touched child Wren — and the mystery has a face.

**XP/leveling note:** the party should reach **level 2** at the end of Act I, by milestone.

# Act II — The Gathering Choir

*Levels 2–4. Hub-and-spoke. Goal: widen the catastrophe from "one strange marsh" to "the whole
Reach is coming apart," let the party build a theory of what's happening, and make the Choir a
real, racing, recruiting rival rather than a faceless enemy.*

**Structure.** Over the days after the first star, **more stars fall** — four further sites
across the Reach of Hollowmere. The party can visit them **in any order**. The Choir reaches
each site first or close behind; the party is always playing catch-up, and what they find
depends on how long the shard (and the Choir) has been working. Use the **clock** below.

**The Choir Clock.** Each site the party does *not* reach in time is one the Choir clears —
they take the shard to the Orrery and add it to the convergence. Track shards-gathered:
- **0–1 Choir-gathered:** Act III's binding is strong; the convergence is stoppable with a
  fight.
- **2–3 Choir-gathered:** the convergence is well underway by Act III; the Orrery is a running
  disaster.
- **All 4 Choir-gathered:** Act III opens *mid-reconvergence* — the worst case, and a great one.

This is the engine that makes the world move without the player (Standard 11). Don't hide the
clock's effects: every site the Choir beats them to should produce a *visible* change — a column
of silver light on the horizon, a Hollowmere family gone to "join the singing," a Choir patrol
on a road that was empty last week.

**The Hub — Hollowmere as base.** Between sites the party returns to Hollowmere, which
deteriorates over the act: more townsfolk hearing the song, the Reeve's patience thinning,
Magister Vesk making contact once the party has seen enough to be worth the risk.

## Chapter 2.1 — The Star-Touched Child (the connective thread)

Not a site — a person, and the spine of Act II. **Wren** is a Hollowmere child in whom a shard
landed *coherent*: instead of building a monster, this fragment lodged in a living mind and
began, haltingly, to *speak*. Wren is still Wren — but sometimes a second voice surfaces, older
than any language, and says true things about the sky.

**Running Wren:**
- The Voice is not omniscient and not obviously kind. It knows it is *broken* and *scattered*.
  It knows it is being *called down*, and it does not want to be. It does not know it was a
  warden — that memory is in other shards. It can confirm small truths (where a star fell, that
  the Choir's gathering is "wrong," that "the door must stay shut") and it withholds or genuinely
  cannot answer the big one (*what door?*).
- **Keep the Voice ambiguous to the very end.** Every helpful thing it says should also be
  consistent with it being a lure. Resolve nothing. (Standard 13: protect the discovery.)
- Wren is in danger from *everyone*: the Choir wants Wren (a coherent shard is their prize), Vesk
  wants Wren contained, the town is starting to whisper. The party's relationship with Wren is
  the emotional core; how they protect, use, or fail Wren is the act's real measure.

**Beat:** the party realizes Wren is connected to the stars, and has to decide what to do about
a frightened child who is also a piece of the thing falling out of the sky. This beat has no
"complete" — it runs the whole act.

## Chapter 2.2 — The Drowned Star (Pale Tarn)

A star fell into one of the cold mountain lakes — the **Pale Tarns**. It did not stop building
when it hit water.

> **READ-ALOUD.** The tarn is a black mirror set in grey stone, and it is the wrong temperature
> — warm mist coils off water that should be snowmelt-cold. Below the surface, a long way down,
> something gives off a slow silver pulse, like a heartbeat seen through deep ice. The shore is
> littered with fish, perfectly arranged smallest to largest, every one of them facing the water.

**The situation.** The submerged shard has rebuilt the drowned — a sunken trapper's lodge's worth
of bodies, now **starspawn husks** that walk the lake bottom and come ashore at the pulse's
rhythm. A handful of **Choristers** (use *cultist*) have made a camp and are trying to raise the
shard with ropes and prayer and drowning themselves by inches to "be near it." Their leader here
is a former Tarn fisher, **Brieth**, who is *gettable* — show the party the grief under the
zealotry (Brieth lost a daughter to the lake years ago and the song sounds like her). A party
that reaches Brieth with empathy rather than steel can turn this whole site without a fight.

**The prize / the cost.** Recovering the shard means going *into* the warm black water (hazard:
cold-shock if the warmth fails; the husks below). Leaving it means the Choir takes it. Either
way, the lake gives up one true thing: at the bottom, fused to the shard, is a **Lantern Order
relic** — a sighting-disk of star-glass that, held to the eye, shows the *lines* connecting every
shard in the Reach back to one point in the mountains: the **Orrery**. (This is the breadcrumb to
Act III, recoverable at any of the four sites in a site-appropriate form.)

## Chapter 2.3 — The Star in the Bramwood (the burning wood)

A star fell in the old **Bramwood** and started a slow, *silver* fire — flames that don't consume
wood so much as *rewrite* it.

> **READ-ALOUD.** The Bramwood is burning without smoke. The fire is the color of moonlight and
> it moves like it's thinking — and where it has passed, the trees are still standing, but
> *changed*: bark gone to grey metal, leaves to thin silver foil that chimes when the wind
> moves, branches reaching in spirals all bent the same way, toward a glow at the wood's heart.

**The situation.** The shard here has reached into the forest itself. The hazard is the
**reforged wood**: animate, slow-moving **starspawn** in the shape of trees and the things that
lived in them (treat the worst as an *awakened tree* / *twig blight* swarm reskinned in silver;
keep most of it environmental dread rather than a fight). A foppish minor noble, **Lord Aldric
Penhall**, has ridden out from his estate with hired swords to *claim* the star as a wonder for
his collection, and is in well over his head — a comedic-then-tragic figure who can become an
ally, a casualty, or a rival depending on play. The Choir is here too, treating the burning wood
as a holy sign.

**The prize / the cost.** The shard at the heart is reachable only through the changed wood, and
the longer the party takes elsewhere, the larger the silver fire grows — make this the site that
*visibly spreads* toward Hollowmere if neglected, your clearest "the world is moving" pressure.

## Chapter 2.4 — The Star Beneath Penhall (the estate / the dig)

A star fell on the **Penhall estate** weeks before the others — and the Penhalls, being who they
are, *dug it up* and brought it indoors. By the time the party hears of it, the great house has
gone quiet.

> **READ-ALOUD.** Penhall House stands with every window lit and no one at the windows. The front
> doors are open. Inside, the silver light comes from the cellar, up through the floorboards in
> thin lines, and the whole house hums one sustained note, the way a wineglass sings under a wet
> finger. On the grand stair, the family portraits have all turned to face the cellar door.

**The situation.** This is the *dungeon* of Act II: a great house remade from the cellar up by a
shard that has had weeks to work and a whole household to work with. The Penhall family and staff
are husks now, arranged through the rooms in tableaux of their last ordinary moments — a dinner
that never ended, a card game still dealt. Play it as exploration-horror with husks that *wake*
when the party disturbs the scene they're frozen in. At the cellar's heart: the most-developed
shard the party has yet seen, nearly a *body*, and the Choir's most advanced cell — including a
**cult fanatic**, **Sister Onye**, who is Canticle Vehr's lieutenant and the party's first taste
of the Choir's true believers at the top.

**The prize / the cost.** This shard is the keystone — whoever holds it has the clearest claim
to the convergence. Sister Onye will flee with it if the fight turns, leaving the party a choice:
chase her toward the Orrery (accelerating Act III) or hold the house and lose the shard to the
Choir. There is no clean win. Lord Aldric Penhall, if alive from 2.3, has a personal stake here —
his family; play it for everything it's worth.

## Chapter 2.5 — The Lantern in the Dark (Magister Vesk)

Once the party has visited **two** sites, **Magister Oraine Vesk** makes contact — a half-elf
astronomer, last of the Lantern Order, who has been watching the party decide whether they're
worth the risk of being seen.

**What Vesk gives the party (rationed across as many scenes as you like):**
- The name **Astera**, the Sky-Warden, and the *shape* of the truth: a dead celestial,
  scattered, being called down by a shard the Order bound under the Orrery long ago.
- The fact that the binding is **failing**, and that Vesk does not know how to fix it — only
  that the last Order did, and bought the Reach three hundred quiet years.
- The warning that the Choir does not understand what they're gathering: that a mind rebuilt by
  worshippers will not come back as what it was. **Vesk is the voice of "re-bind, don't wake."**
- What Vesk *won't* say plainly (and may not fully know): what Astera was guarding *against*.
  Vesk is afraid the answer is in the song, and afraid to listen closely enough to learn it.

**Vesk is wary, not warm.** Make the party earn this. Vesk has survived by hiding; trusting
adventurers is a risk Vesk takes only because the alternative is the Choir winning by default.

## Act II climax — The Convergence Becomes Visible

However the four sites resolve, Act II ends when the party understands three things:
1. **All the shards are being pulled to one place** — the Orrery, in the Pale Tarns.
2. **The Choir intends to put Astera back together there** — the "Reconvergence" — and believes
   it will wake a god.
3. **They are running out of time.** The sky over the Pale Tarns has gone *bright*; the song can
   be heard in Hollowmere now, in daylight; and the Choir's road is open.

End the act on the party committing to go up the mountain — and, ideally, on a genuine decision
already forced about **Wren** (do they bring the child, hide the child, or has the Choir already
taken the child?). The party should be **level 4** entering Act III.

# Act III — The Reconvergence

*Levels 4–5. Anchored, climactic. Goal: bring the party to the Orrery, force the central
decision the whole campaign has been asking, and let their answer — not a die roll — decide what
wakes, or doesn't, under the breaking sky.*

## Chapter 3.1 — The Road Up

The climb to the **Orrery** through the Pale Tarns. Use this chapter to:
- **Resolve the clock.** Every shard the Choir gathered in Act II is now a candle burning at the
  Orrery ahead; the more they took, the further along the reconvergence already is, and the more
  the mountain itself has begun to change (the silver creep from the star-fall sites, meeting
  here).
- **Spend the relationships.** Anyone the party turned in Act II — Brieth, Lord Aldric, a
  reformed Chorister — can arrive here to pay it back. Anyone they failed can arrive as a husk,
  or as a believer at the gate. The road up is where Act II's choices come due.
- **Last words with Wren / the Voice.** If Wren is with the party, the Voice grows clearer and
  more afraid the higher they climb — and finally tells the party the one thing it has been
  unable to say: *"I was the lock. They are trying to make me a key."* Let this land without
  explaining it. (It is the closest the campaign comes to handing over the answer — and it still
  doesn't say what the door holds.)

## Chapter 3.2 — The Orrery

> **READ-ALOUD.** The Orrery was built to watch the sky and ended up holding a piece of it down.
> Rings of black stone and tarnished bronze stand open to the air, turning — slowly, wrongly,
> against the wind — and at their center a shaft drops into the mountain to where the first shard
> was bound. Above, the sky is not the sky. It is *close*. The stars over the Orrery are sliding
> together, slow and certain, toward a single point of silver fire directly overhead, and the
> song is no longer a tune. It is a voice, and it is almost whole, and it is trying to remember
> how to speak.

**The situation.** **Canticle Vehr** — former Lantern astronomer, now the Choir's prophet — has
gathered the Choir's shards here and is conducting the Reconvergence: feeding shard after shard
into the failing binding-shaft, calling Astera down to be reassembled. Vehr is not a fool and not
a liar — Vehr *believes*, with the conviction of someone who has read the old Order's books and
chosen to ignore their warnings. *Stat note: Vehr is a tougher cult fanatic — give a* mage *or*
priest *frame as fits your party's level, with the song as a recharge "command" effect that can
turn husks against the party each round.* Around Vehr: the surviving Choristers, the gathered
shards' half-built **starspawn**, and whatever the party's clock has already woken.

**The confrontation is a conversation as much as a fight.** Vehr will *talk* — will try to
recruit the party into the most important moment in the history of the Reach. Vehr's argument is
genuinely tempting: *the sky is broken; here is a chance to make it whole and be remembered for
it.* The party's likely counter — *we don't know what we're waking* — is exactly the warning
Vehr has chosen to disbelieve.

## Chapter 3.3 — What Wakes

The climax forces **the central choice**. The party's answer should be the product of everything
they've learned and decided — not a skill check. Build whichever of these they steer toward, and
honor it:

- **Stop the Reconvergence — destroy the shards.** Sever the song. This means breaking the
  binding-shaft and letting the gathered shards burn out — a final, irreversible *silencing* of
  Astera. The Reach is saved and a little poorer for it; the door (whatever it was) is now held
  shut by nothing but distance and luck. If Wren carries a coherent shard, *this choice asks
  whether Wren survives it* — the campaign's hardest question, and one the party, not the dice,
  should answer. Best paired with an earned cost.
- **Complete the Reconvergence — and shape the waking.** Let Astera return — but wrest the
  *shaping* away from Vehr. Whoever the party has been to the shards all campaign (the empathy
  they showed the husks' origins, the care they gave Wren, the truths they chose to honor) is
  what Astera wakes *remembering*. A party that has been merciful and curious can wake a warden;
  a party that has been only violent can wake exactly the murdered god Vehr would have made.
  Make the prior play *matter* here, visibly. The door gets its lock back — and a new warden who
  remembers the people who made it.
- **Re-bind the shard — kick it down the road.** Use the Lantern relic and Vesk's fragments of
  the old binding to put Astera *back to sleep*. Survivable, repeatable, and a confession: the
  party becomes the new Lantern Order, and buys the Reach another three hundred years and the
  same unanswered question. Hauntingly, the *best-understood* ending and the one that resolves
  the least.

**Vehr's fate** and **Wren's fate** are sub-decisions inside the above — don't pre-script them.
A reformed Chorister, a turned Brieth, a surviving Lord Aldric can all bend the climax. The one
hard rule: **do not reveal what Astera was guarding against.** The party may save the Reach and
still never learn what door they kept shut, or opened, or left to luck. That unanswered question
is the campaign's last gift. (Standard 14: the world is not the party's to fully author — and
some mysteries keep.)

## Aftermath

Close on Hollowmere. The song is gone, or changed, or distant. The frogs are back, or they
aren't. Whatever the party chose, the Reach remembers them as the people who were here when the
sky came apart — and either put it back, put it down, or put it off. Seed one quiet hook for
whatever comes next: a single star, very high, very still, that wasn't there before. The DM
decides whether it's falling.

# Appendix B — Skyfall Iron (the star-metal)

A fist-sized chunk of a shard, prised loose, is a recurring plot object and a temptation.

- **Warm, humming, faintly luminous.** It does not cool. In darkness it sheds dim silver light
  out to 5 ft.
- **The song travels with it.** Carrying a piece means the Choir (and worse) can *find* the
  bearer; it draws husks and Choristers toward the party. A blessing and a beacon.
- **It wants to build.** Left near organic matter (a wound, a corpse, a sleeping creature) for
  hours, it *reaches* — minor at first (a healed-wrong scar, a too-vivid dream of the sky), worse
  with time. Useful pressure on a party that hoards it.
- **Attunement temptation (optional).** A spellcaster who attunes to a chunk (treat as a minor
  wondrous item) can, once per long rest, *hear the song clearly* to ask the Voice one yes/leaning
  question about the shards — at the cost of a point of contact with the reconvergence each use
  (DM-tracked dread; never free). Offer it; never make it safe.

# Appendix C — Starspawn (the failed drafts)

Not a single monster — a *category* of the shard's discards. Build each by reskinning an SRD
block in silver and wrongness:

- **Husk** — *zombie.* A person the shard rebuilt and abandoned. Slow, relentless, recognizable.
  On death: a one-turn chime of biting motes (*swarm of insects*, disperses next round).
- **Chorus-thing** — *swarm of insects* or *swarm of ravens* reskinned as a cloud of silver
  motes that hums; the song's teeth.
- **Reforged beast** — any beast block (*wolf*, *giant frog*, *brown bear*) with bark-to-metal,
  eyes-to-silver-beads cosmetics and a faint chime when it moves. Wilderness sites.
- **Reforged wood** — *awakened tree* / *twig blight* in silver foil and grey-metal bark.
  Bramwood only; mostly environmental.
- **Near-body** (Penhall cellar; the Orrery) — the most-developed shard; build as a *flesh
  golem* or, at the climax, a bespoke threat scaled to a level-5 party. The reconvergence
  *almost* succeeding.

Keep the cosmetics consistent — silver light, a chime, a spiral, the song — so players learn to
read "the star did this" at a glance, across every site, without being told.

## DM Quick Reference — Beats by Act

**Act I (lv 1→2):** accept the job → hear the real song → reach the crash site → survive the
trapper-husks → learn the Choir wants this → glimpse Wren.

**Act II (lv 2→4):** four star-fall sites in any order (Drowned Star, Bramwood, Penhall House,
+ the clock) → connect with/protect Wren → earn Vesk and the name *Astera* → recover the relic
pointing to the Orrery → realize the Choir is converging there → commit to the mountain.

**Act III (lv 4→5):** the road up (relationships come due) → the Orrery and Vehr → the central
choice: **silence / shape / re-bind** → aftermath, and one new star that wasn't there before.

*Keep the mystery alive to the last scene. The sky is the gift — protect it.*
